In Chapter I, a coastal storm wrecked a ship near the village. One passenger survived — tall, silent, clutching a letter addressed to someone twenty years dead. Elara, who hears voices in the waves, helped pull him from the surf.

Chapter I opens on an unseasonal storm striking an unnamed coastal village with unusual ferocity. We meet Elara, the lighthouse keeper's daughter, who has carried since childhood a strange sensitivity: she hears voices in the waves. Her grandmother first named this gift, and it has shaped her isolation ever since.

As Elara climbs the lighthouse to tend the lantern, the waves begin screaming — which she understands as a warning. Moments later, she witnesses a ship founder on the rocks offshore. Fishermen drag a single survivor from the surf: a tall man, foreign, with rope-worn hands and deep-water eyes. He is carrying a letter. The envelope is addressed, in faded ink, to a woman the village buried twenty years ago.

Chapter II picks up the following morning. The stranger is awake, sitting upright in Elara's spare room, staring at the wall. He has folded the letter into a careful square and placed it deliberately under the oil lamp. Elara tries to reach him in three languages. He doesn't respond until she speaks an old coastal dialect her grandmother taught her, half-forgotten, rough as salt. He blinks. Once. Slowly. As if acknowledging a debt.

The man they pulled from the surf spoke no language anyone in the village recognized. He was tall — unusually so — with hands roughened by rope-work and eyes the color of deep water. The doctor said he was perhaps forty. Elara thought he looked older than the sea itself.

She found him in the morning, sitting up in the spare room, staring at the wall as though reading something written there that no one else could see. He had folded the letter into a perfect square and placed it beneath the oil lamp, and something in the deliberateness of the gesture told her he did not want it discussed.

"You're safe," she said in three languages. He turned to look at her only when she said it in the fourth — an old coastal dialect her grandmother had taught her, half-forgotten, rough as salt on the tongue. His expression did not change, but he blinked. Once. Slowly. As if acknowledging a debt.
